The signal

Pricing the trough as permanent — not the decade of 8% CAGR

Infosys compounded USD revenue at 8.3% CAGR over five years, sustained 21% operating margins through multiple cycles, and generated record FCF in FY25–FY26. The market, at its own ~11.5% discount rate, prices 3.67% perpetual FCF growth — a number indistinguishable from the FY24 CC trough of 1.4%. The shaded band is the gap the thesis must close: the bear says FY24's trough is the new normal; the bull says the $14.9bn TCV backlog and AI-legacy modernisation demand reopen the historical corridor.

The door: adaptation is real but primarily defensive

P1 / P2 · −14% / −17%

Infosys is adapting — Topaz Fabric (Nov 2025) integrates 50+ purpose-built AI agents across IT ops, quality engineering, and cybersecurity with a human-in-the-loop architecture. The company launched 200+ enterprise AI agents with Google Cloud (May 2025) and announced a strategic collaboration with OpenAI (May 2026) targeting enterprise AI transformation. AI services annualised revenue crossed $1bn (~5.5% of total, Q3 FY26), growing quarter-on-quarter. Chairman Nandan Nilekani committed a $1bn investment programme (AI platforms, capability, co-innovation) at the June 2026 AGM. But the critical earnings call admission changes the framing: CFO Jayesh Sanghrajka confirmed that AI productivity gains are "largely" passed back to clients due to competitive intensity — not retained as margin. Kotak Institutional Equities independently pegged the sector-wide pricing deflation at 3–5% annually on renewals. Infosys uses AI to do existing work faster, then competes that saving away. That is P2's structural leg. The distinction from Persistent is decisive: Persistent's SASVA generates genuinely net-new revenue (the P1a/R5 pair cancel at −14%/+14%). INFY's Topaz primarily optimises existing delivery at competitive cost; the net-new revenue (5.5% ARR) is growing but hasn't yet broken out of the efficiency-compression gravity.

The real opportunity: legacy modernisation and agentic integration

R1 / R4 · +7% / +2.4%

Your framing is evidenced: AI does not cleanly replace complex, multi-system, regulated work. Infosys management confirmed on the FY26 earnings call that legacy modernisation in banks — migrating older-generation technology with foundation models — is where clients are "adopting AI quickly." The six-area AI framework explicitly includes Agentic Legacy Modernization ("reverse-engineer existing estates, understand their intent, progressively modernize without disruption") — exactly the complex, multi-phase work that requires a trusted large-scale integrator. The GlobalFoundries deal (Jun 2026) demonstrates the emerging outcome-based model: billed on operational improvements (incident reduction, resilience, user experience) rather than headcount, shifting from a volume-sensitive revenue model toward realization-based. If this replicates at scale, it resolves P2 structurally. Financial Services and Energy/Utilities grew above company average in FY26, driven by exactly this type of deep-integration work. The addressable market framing is credible: the Nasscom-McKinsey cited $300–400bn AI services TAM by 2030 specifically includes the enterprise data engineering, governance, and integration work that is not automated away.

The margin expansion thesis — R5 in detail

R5 · +6%

The market prices 21% EBIT margin as a permanent ceiling; the thesis says it's a floor with a gradient. Three sources stack: Project Maximus contributes ~30–40bps/quarter of gross efficiency, currently reinvested into AI talent and sales (40bps reinvestment rate). Once the $1bn AI capability build matures (FY28 horizon), that spend normalises and retention rises. AI delivery tools internally — if 25–30% of productivity gain is retained (not passed through), at ₹1,78,650 Cr revenue the annual value is ~₹1,340 Cr extra EBIT at steady state, or ~75bps. Operating leverage on fixed overhead adds 20–25bps as revenue-per-employee drifts from $61,500 (FY26) toward $65,000+ without equivalent headcount growth. Conservative aggregate: +100–120bps by FY29 (22.1% target), adding ~₹2,341 Cr EBIT (₹1,685 Cr after-tax FCF). At the terminal spread, this contributes ₹9,457 Cr PV — ₹23/share from TV alone, with explicit-period FCF uplift adding more. The CFO guided that pricing is "accretive" with AI and Project Maximus contributing "positively" — the marginal retention is real even if competitors partially erode it.

The deal pipeline — R2 in detail

R2 · +10%

The $14.9bn large-deal TCV in FY26 — a 28% increase over FY25 — is contractually signed. It is not pipeline speculation. With 55% net-new (vs churn replacement), this represents genuine new client relationships and scope. The key risk is conversion speed: "the market focuses less on order inflows and more on conversion speed — how quickly signed contracts translate into revenue growth." Management confirmed that deal adjacency is common: clients expanding scope within large engagements, providing organic upsell above the headline TCV. The $650M / 6.5yr incremental US deal ($125M ACV) announced separately from Q4 is ramping from Q2 FY27. Top-50 clients increased spend: $50M+ clients at 85, $100M+ clients at 41, $400M+ increased by 2 in FY26. This is not a pipeline that collapses to zero in the bear case — it anchors a floor on the revenue trajectory.

R3 · +5%
